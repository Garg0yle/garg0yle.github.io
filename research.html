Projects

We study neural circuits in the basal ganglia, a multifunctional brain region that plays a role in the regulation of movement, learning, motivation, and reward. Ongoing projects include:

1. Study of neural circuits in the external segment of the globus pallidus (GPe):

We recently discovered that transgenic mouse lines could be used to identify distinct cell types in the GPe that differ in their spatial distribution, intrinsic physiology, and axonal projection patterns (Mastro et al., 2014).

Lhx6-GPe neurons are most concentrated in the medial portions of the GPe while PV-GPe neurons are most concentrated in the lateral portions of the GPe. Using viral expression of fluorescent proteins, we were able to trace the extensive axonal projections of Lhx6- and PV-GPe neurons which targeted nuclei in the basal ganglia, thalamus, midbrain, and cortex.

Both Lhx6- and PV-GPe neurons project to many of the same basal ganglia nuclei, but the density of their projection patterns to these nuclei differ. Lhx6-GPe neurons more strongly target the striatum and substantia nigra compact (SNc) and PV-GPe neurons more strongly target the STN and parafasciular nucleus of the thalamus.

Current experiments are investigating the synaptic and functional properties of Lhx6- and PV-GPe circuits, with the goal of discovering their role in behavior and disease.

Distinct subpopulations in the GPe subdivide the neuronal populations and are distributed differently along the medial lateral axis.
